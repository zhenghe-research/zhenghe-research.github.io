VSMC Phenotypic Switch Modulators

Vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs) are highly plastic cells capable of transitioning between a quiescent contractile phenotype and a proliferative synthetic phenotype — a switch central to the pathogenesis of atherosclerosis and in-stent restenosis.

My primary doctoral project defines the transcriptional basis of this switch through a multi-stage computational and experimental framework:

  1. Discovery — Using a donor-aware limma model on GSE193817 (284 samples, 148 donors, 136 paired), I identified a 1,103-gene switching program (884 UP, 219 DN; FDR < 0.05) capturing a proliferation-versus-cholesterol-metabolism transcriptional axis.

  2. Transferability validation — The program demonstrates context-selective transferability across multiple independent perturbation datasets (growth-factor/cytokine contexts: 5/5 concordant; metabolic/stress contexts: 0/4), validated in GSE69637 and GSE253003.

  3. Human plaque anchoring — The program is anchored to human atherosclerotic plaque states using single-cell data (GSE155512), with strong canonical SMC enrichment confirmed.

  4. Experimental validation — A PDGF-BB induction model in primary human aortic SMCs (HAoSMC) uses resveratrol as a pharmacological counter-stimulus to test whether the computationally defined program is functionally and pharmacologically responsive (partial resveratrol rescue endpoint).

Target journal: Arteriosclerosis, Thrombosis, and Vascular Biology (ATVB)

Key tools: R (limma, WGCNA, clusterProfiler, GSVA), Western Blot, Immunofluorescence, RT-qPCR

ABCB5⁺ MSCs & Chronic Wound Healing

ABCB5⁺ MSCs are a rare population of skin-derived stromal cells defined by expression of the ATP-binding cassette transporter ABCB5, with potent immunomodulatory and tissue-regenerative properties.

This line of research has two components:

Published review — A comprehensive review of the differentiation and regeneration potential of ABCB5⁺ MSCs and their clinical translation perspectives, published in Journal of Clinical Medicine (2025).

Original bioinformatics study — A systems-level, multi-modal analysis of the paracrine role of ABCB5⁺ MSCs in chronic wound repair, structured around an expression-derived paracrine candidate atlas integrated with:

  • Chronic wound transcriptome profiling (WGCNA co-expression analysis)
  • Convergent target prioritization (M1 atlas ∩ wound DEG modules → 10 hub genes)
  • Single-cell RNA-seq contextualization + cell-cell communication analysis (CellChat)
  • Immune infiltration profiling (ssGSEA)
  • Mendelian randomization boundary testing
  • Molecular docking for structural plausibility

The analysis converges on three tissue-local repair axes: ECM remodeling (DCN, FBLN1, FBLN2, MFAP4, SERPINF1), complement-mediated immunoregulation (C3, CFH, SERPING1), and CXCL12-driven chemotactic crosstalk. The manuscript is under final preparation for submission, with parallel wet-lab co-culture validation underway.

Endocytic Trafficking in HeLa Cells

Working within the Starkuvienė Lab at BioQuant, I investigate endocytic pathway regulation in HeLa Kyoto cells using:

  • Size-selective dextran-FITC uptake assays (10/40/70 kDa) — fluid-phase endocytosis quantification across molecular weight ranges
  • Pathway-specific pharmacological inhibition — Wortmannin (PI3K-dependent macropinocytosis) and Dynasore (dynamin-dependent endocytosis) to dissect pathway contributions
  • 3D spheroid culture models — Nunclon Sphera ultra-low attachment platform for validating endocytic behaviour in structurally relevant 3D context
  • Wound healing migration assays — scratch assay with quantitative imaging analysis

This work contributes to the lab’s broader programme on how spatial and temporal regulation of intracellular trafficking is achieved and how its dysregulation contributes to disease and aging.
